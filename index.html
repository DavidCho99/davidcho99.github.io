The Original Path: Deep Dive into Research

Initially, my sights were set on academia. My goal was to pursue a Master's or Ph.D., which led me to immerse myself deeply in undergraduate research. At the LAIM³ lab, I focused on structural learning and causal interpretability, building Bayesian network architectures to process massive healthcare datasets on HPC clusters. I loved the mathematical rigor of research, and my dedication even led to an invitation to pursue a Ph.D. with my university's Math Finance group.

The Turning Point: The AI Revolution

While the Ph.D. offer was a such honor, I couldn't ignore what was happening outside the lab. Watching how rapidly AI is transforming the industry triggered a massive shift in my perspective. I realized that the most immediate, profound impact is happening in the field—where complex models are actually engineered, scaled, and deployed to solve real-world problems. There were practical, scalable engineering concepts I knew I could only learn by working directly in the industry, and I wanted to be out there building.

The Pivot: Engineering for the Real World

Turning down the Ph.D. track to shift my focus toward the tech industry felt like a late pivot, but my desire to learn and build in a fast-paced environment completely took over. I redirected my career path toward software and machine learning engineering. I wanted to become a developer who doesn't just understand the theoretical math behind the models, but also knows how to build real applications and services that deliver a genuine impact to people.
